Research

Research Interests

We are interested in developing and applying novel s pectroscopic reporters, methods, and techniques to study the structure-dynamics-function (or reactivity) relationship of various chemical and biological systems, such as peptides and proteins, with a focus on assessing the kinetic and mechanistic details of the chemical/biological processes and/or reactions in question. Our current research efforts include, but are not limited to, (1) development of unnatural amino acid-based, environmentally-sensitive vibrational probes for site-specific interrogation of protein structure, hydration, electrostatics, dynamics, and function，(2) development of unnatural amino acid-based fluorescence reporters for biological spectroscopy and microscopy，(3)development of unnatural amino acid-based fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) or photoinduced electron transfer (PET) pairs for characterization of protein conformations, conformational change, and conformational dynamics, (4) development of biological materials, such as fibrils, that have tailored spectroscopic or optical properties, (5) assessment of the kinetics and mechanism of protein folding under in vitro and in vivo conditions, (6) site-specific interrogation of the molecular interactions between protein sidechains and naturally-occurring osmolytes as well as ions. (7) manipulation of biological electron and proton transfer processes via site-selective tuning of the underlying driving force, (8) development of multidimensional spectroscopic and imaging techniques for chemical, biological, and physical applications...

Two-Dimensional Infrared Spectroscopy

To gain higher spectral resolution, ultrafast dynamical information, and coupling mechanisms between individual transitions, we employ two-dimensional infrared spectroscopy (2D IR). 2D IR is based on a three-pulse excitation scheme using femtosecond IR laser excitation where the signal field S(τ,T,t) is measured with fixed waiting time T but varying τ and t. Fourier transforms along τ and t give a 2D IR spectrum. The detailed shapes of the diagonal and cross-peaks in the spectrum can then be used to study many different phenomena such as mode coupling, internal energy reorganization, and other relaxation properties of the distributions of states.

Temperature-Jump and Transient Infrared Spectroscopy

We are interested in observing some of the fastest events in folding. To this end, we apply laser induced temperture-jump and photochemical triggering to rapidly initiate folding/unfolding reactions, which we can monitor by probing various infrared absorption reporters that can measure either global or local dynamics. Combining a range of different such observables allows us to build mechanistic pictures of the folding "pathways" of some of the fastest folding proteins.

Fluorescence Correlation Spectroscopy and Single-Molecule FRET

How do peptides interact with membranes? What are the size distributions and time scales associated with aggregate formation? Can we understand the structure and dynamics of intrinsicly dissordered peptides? These are all burning questions with the potential to answer pressing needs in the application of anti-microbials and amyloid-related diseases. Single molecule methods go a long way towards probing the micro-heterogeneity and dynamics associated with these processes.

Non-Natural Amino Acid IR and FRET Probes

Design and application of new, small, IR and UV chromophores allows us to probe systems with minimal perturbation and high structural resolution.

Nanosecond transient IR

As shown (Schematic diagram of the nanosecond transient IR setup), the transient IR absorption apparatus consists of a nanosecond pump source and a continuous wave (CW) quantum cascade (QC) IR laser (Daylight Solutions, CA) to serve as the probe source. For the laser-induced temperature-jump (T-jump) experiments, the wavelength of the pump pulse (∼3 ns) was tuned to ∼1.9 μm via Raman shifting the fundamental output of a Nd:YAG laser (Infinity, Coherent Radiation, CA) using H2. For the other transient IR measurements discussed, the third harmonic of the Minilite II Nd:YAG laser (Continuum Electrooptic, Inc., CA) was used as the pump pulse. The pump-induced change in the probe signal was measured by a 50 MHz liquid nitrogen cooled HgCdTe (MCT) detector (Kolmar Technologies, MA), and signal digitization was accomplished by a digital oscilloscope (Tektronix, OR).

Chemical Physics of Defects in Nanocrystals

As shown (Schematic diagram of the FCS setup), the excitation light was derived from either an argon ion laser (Spectra-Physics, CA) or a helium–neon laser (JDS Uniphase Corporation, CA), depending on the fluorophore used. Light focusing was achieved by a confocal microscope (Nikon Eclipse TE 300) equipped with an oil immersion objective (100× or 60×), and the confocal volume was defined by a 50 μm pinhole. A Hanbury-Brown and Twiss setup, which involves a 50/50 nonpolarizing beamsplitter (Newport, CA) and two avalanche photodiodes (APDs) (PerkinElmer, NJ), was used to increase the time resolution. Cross-correlation was accomplished by a Flex 03-LQ-01 autocorrelation card (Correlator.com, NJ).
